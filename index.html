The goal of message design is to transmit information to the receiver as effectively as possible. Logic and emotion cannot exist without each other.

01
The Basis
Sender → Receiver
Attention → Retention

How is a message received?

The process of communication follows a strict path from the sender to the receiver. A good design not only attracts attention — it ensures the message is correctly understood and remembered. Form and content must be perfectly aligned to create impact.

"Theory and practice cannot exist without each other."
01
Attention
The message must first attract attention. Without this initial hook, the entire communication chain fails before it begins.
02
Legibility
If a text is too difficult or unattractively designed, the reader drops out. Visual clarity is not optional — it is structural.
03
Understanding
The receiver must be able to interpret the information correctly. Ambiguity is the enemy of comprehension.
04
Retention
The ability to remember the information. A message that cannot be recalled has not truly been communicated.
Chapter Two

The Three
Pillars of
Design

Within message design, three disciplines work closely together: Graphic Design, Information Design, and Instruction Design.

Three disciplines.
One message.

Message design is not a single discipline — it is the intersection of three fields, each contributing its own logic to the transmission of knowledge. Their overlap is where effective communication lives.

01 — Graphic Design
Aesthetics & Atmosphere

Focuses on visual aesthetics, color, and typography to evoke emotion and create the affective dimension of the message.

02 — Information Design
Clarity & Structure

Ensures the structure, clarity, and logic of data so that it is easily findable, navigable, and understandable by the reader.

03 — Instruction Design
Action & Outcome

Focuses on action: how do you help the user perform a specific task correctly? Design as a directive force.

Form must strictly follow function. Fully understand the core problem before a single line is drawn.

Organize information in a logical hierarchy. A systematic framework guides the user without confusion.

Eliminate ambiguity. Every visual element must actively contribute to the immediate understanding of the message.

Strip away the non-essential. Minimalism is not removing content — it is optimizing the space between meaning and decoration.

Ensure cohesive relationships between all design components. Unity builds absolute trust and stylistic harmony.

Information Access

Structure layouts to lower cognitive friction. Information must be instantly searchable and accessible.

Case Study 01 — Wayfinding

"A sign is only effective when it eliminates alternative choices."

The ultimate test of Message Design occurs in complex physical spaces — international airports and transit hubs. Wayfinding relies entirely on the immediate, unambiguous transmission of data.

By isolating critical directional markers and removing visual noise from surrounding architecture, the environment becomes intuitive. Abstract geographic data transforms into clear, actionable instruction.

Case Study 02 — Data Visualization

"To clarify is not to simplify, but to optimize."

Raw numbers carry no inherent narrative — they require a structured visual environment to become meaningful information. The law of elimination becomes paramount: ruthlessly strip away decorative elements to let the data speak for itself.

High contrast is applied exclusively to the most critical variables, forcing the reader's eye to immediately register the core discovery without cognitive friction.

Case Study 03 — Editorial Architecture

Chronography: the page as cinematic sequence.

A magazine should not be a series of static, isolated layouts, but a fluid cinematic sequence. By manipulating text density and the strategic placement of white space, a designer controls the exact speed at which a reader consumes information.

Heavy, dense columns slow the gaze for deep focus. Explosive typographic statements and generous negative space accelerate pacing — creating an intentional psychological rhythm throughout.

The responsibility of the designer.

Message design is fundamentally an act of discipline. As technologies advance and the velocity of information accelerates, the human cognitive capacity remains unchanged. To practice design responsibly is to reject decoration in favor of utility, and complexity in favor of clarity.
